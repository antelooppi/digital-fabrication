Assignment 11: Computer-Controlled Machining

Introduction

This week I designed and fabricated a stool using Recontech 1312 CNC milling machine. I used FreeCAD to make a three-piece parametric design, and 14.7mm thick sheet of plywood to cut the individual pieces for it. The final stool consists of three parts – the top and two legs – which interlocks to each other using finger joints and pockets. The design is based on our teacher Kris’ Fablab bench design, which I modified to my own taste. The stool was put together using only tightly-fitted joints and pockets, so no glue or any other enhancement was needed.

Designing the Stool in FreeCAD

I started the design process by defining the variables for stool height, top diameter and material thickness inside FreeCAD’s spreadsheet. I used these three variables in different mathematical forms for all the dimensions. I started the drawing process by making an initial shape for the leg without any joints or slots. I then copied the base twice to make the individual leg designs. I added identical top joints to both of them, and mirror-imaged slots in the middle for interlocking the legs together in 90 degree angle. I then added the top part, to which I cut pockets using boolean operation. After refining the design and its dimensions (including measuring the actual material thickness), I exported the SVG file using FreeCAD’s Techdraw workbench and processed the file in Inkscape, similar to the laser cutting process.

Cutting the Material

I used 120x120cm plywood with 14.7mm thickness to cut the parts for the stool. I started by securing the material to the sacrificial layer using composite nails. The sacrificial layer should usually be porous enough to lock the material to it using only the machine’s vacuum. I couldn’t manage to get enough pressure from it, so I used composite nails in conjunction with clamps to attach the sacrificial layer and the plywood to the machine bed securely.

After securing the materials to the machine bed, I installed the cutter and calibrated its Z axis using an integrated calibration tool, which calculates the Z origin automatically when the milling bit touches it. I then set the X and Y origins using Mach3 CNC program and imported my design file into VCarve Pro. In VCarve, I defined some basic job setups - like job size, job type, Z zero position and XY datum position – as well as fillets and pocket setups. In the same program, I also defined tool-specific setup, like feed rates, chip load and number of flutes. Finally, I added some tabs to the job to keep the parts in place during the milling, exported the toolpaths to Mach3, and executed the cutting job.

Cleaning Up the Parts

After the CNC job was done, the individual parts were still attached to the plywood sheet by the tabs which held them in place during the milling. I used Makita handheld power tool with cutter bit for separating the individual parts from the sheet. I then used a saw and a power sander to remove the remaining tabs from the stool parts. Finally, I used regular sandpaper with some good old elbow grease to smoothen all the rough edges and to make the overall impression nicer. To my delight, all the pockets and joints fitted together tightly, so I didn’t need to use glue or any other enhancement to put the stool together. The top part needed a little bit of love from the gavel when attaching it to the legs, but after that the stool was assembled and could already bare sitting-weight.

Reflection

With this assignment, I learned the basics of 3-axis CNC machining. I think I need to go through the process at least couple of times to actually start to see all the possibilities the CNC machine can offer to my builds. When making the stool, I learned the basics of designing joinery and pockets, and how to mill them effectively. This is really beneficial for me, since basic joinery is a skill I could use almost everywhere – from making boxes to building furniture. I also learned some of the restrictions of the machine, when cutting the joints. Having the basic sense of what the machine can and cannot do is definitely going to help me in the future, when designing something for the CNC machine.
