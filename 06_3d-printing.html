Assignment 06: 3D Printing

My Interests

I am mostly interested in fabricating some functional parts for my instrument designs with 3D printing. The parts could be something like potentiometer knobs, hinges, casings, or nuts and bolts. The ability of fabricating functioning custom parts is going to be really beneficial for me, since that eliminates the need for only relying on parts that already exits in the market. I have more than once ran into the problem of not finding the part I need, which usually leads to compromises I’m not always happy with. With 3D printing, I can finally fabricate the parts just the way I need them.

The Test Print

I started my 3D printing process with printing a guitar pick holder keychain I downloaded from Thingiverse. I wanted to print something small and practical, so that the printing time would remain short. I imported the STL file to PrusaSlicer and sliced it using PLA, 0.20mm layer height, 20% infill without supports. With these settings, I managed to get the printing printing time with Prusa MK4S around six minutes. I continued by exporting the G-code and moved the file to the printer using usb stick. Everything went fine with the printing process and I was happy to see that the printed result actually worked!

My Design

For my own design, I created a custom sized nut and bolt. I wanted to explore the properties of thread making and how it would work with 3D printed objects. I tried out multiple different ways to make a thread in FreeCad, which many of them were hard to manage and felt like were constantly on the edge of breaking the sketch. I ended up using the Fasteners workbench add-on, which was straightforward to use, but still had the key parameters for making my custom design. In a nutshell, I started by making a ThreadedRod inside the Fasteners workbench, which I used for cutting a matching hole to a nut with the cut function inside the Part workbench.

I wanted to explore the fitting of the threads, so I exported four different versions of the same nut design with 0.0mm, 0.1mm, 0.2mm 0.3mm fitting tolerances. I continued by importing the bolt and nuts to PrusaSlicer and sliced them to get estimated time for printing, which was around 1.5 hours. I made my design dimensionally quite small to begin with, so the printing time wasn’t really a problem. I still wanted to optimize the design, so I reduced the unnecessary thickness of the nut and the bolt’s baseplate in FreeCad and made the bolt also shorter. In PrusaSlicer, I noticed the layer height had the biggest affect on the printing time. I ended up picking 0.2mm height, since it was half faster than 0.1mm. The infill percentage had also an effect to the printing time, but nothing near as dramatic. Between 5% and 20% infill, there was only couple minutes of difference, and between 20% and overly exaggerated 90% the difference was around 15 minutes. I had good experience printing the guitar pick holder with 20% infill, so I decided to go with that. The design doesn’t have any major overhangs when printed from the baseplate up, so I didn’t need any supports for my print. The final printing time with one bolt and four nuts was around 1 hour.

The printing process went smoothly and everything worked as intended. I was kind of surprised how much fitting tolerance was necessary to make the threads actually work, since only the largest tolerance of 0.3mm was able to screw into the bolt. I would still like to test larger tolerances in the future just to see if the fit could be better, but for this purpose I think the end result worked nicely.
