Assignment 12: Molding and Casting

Introduction

This week I designed and fabricated a knob for an encoder to use in my final project. I designed the knob in three phases in FreeCAD and used the Roland MDX-40 to mill the master mold of the design. I then used Mold Star pourable silicone rubber kit to cast the negative mold and Smooth-Cast liquid plastic to cast the final result. In the end I had a self-designed and fabricated knob that fits potentiometers and encoders with 6mm D-shaped shaft.

Making the FreeCAD Design

I produced the design in FreeCAD in three phases. First, I made the initial design for the knob. I used the inner dimensions for the encoder I was using in my final project. The encoder has a D-shaped shaft with 6mm diameter. I found this and the other dimensions from the encoder’s datasheet. After doing the initial design, I created another body surrounding the model and used boolean operation to cut the model into the surrounding block. This operation produced the design for the negative mold. I then basically repeated the same steps again, using the dimensions of the actual wax block used for the milling, which gave me the final body to use milling the master mold.

Milling the Master Mold

I produced the master mold from a block of jewelry wax using Roland MDX-40 milling machine. I started by attaching the wax block to the machine bed and imported my design to Vcarve Pro. In Vcarve, I calculated the toolpaths, which consisted of four passes. The first pass was to flatten the surface, the second pass was for the rough cut and the last two were for smoothening the cut.

Casting the Negative Mold

After milling the master mold, I produced a negative silicone mold using Mold Star pourable silicone rubber kit. The kit consists of two bottle of silicone polymer liquids, with curing time of six hours. I carefully stirred both of the liquids in their bottles before mixing them together with 1:1 ratio. I then put the mixture inside a vacuum chamber to “boil” out all the bubbles. This helps with getting rid of all the bubbles from the mixture, and therefore producing a better mold. I poured the mixture into the wax mold and left it to cure overnight. The next morning, I had a negative mold ready to use for the final casting.

Casting the Knob

After finishing the negative mold, I was ready to cast the actual knob. For the casting, I used Smooth-Cast Liquid Plastic polyurethane resin kit. The kit consists of two bottles of liquids with 1:1 mixture ratio. The mixing of the liquids were otherwise similar to the negative mold casting, but this time I didn’t use the vacuum chamber due to a super-short pot life of the kit. I added some black color to the mix, stirred everything, and poured it as a thin line into the silicone mold. I left to cure for 10 minutes, after which I had the finished knob.
